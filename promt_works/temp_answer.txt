Based on information available up to May 2025, Iran's economy has been under severe contractionary pressure, though it has shown signs of limited adaptation. The overall trend has been one of stagnation and shrinkage in real terms for most of the population, characterized by high inflation and currency devaluation.

Here are five main contributing factors:

1.  **International Sanctions:** The primary external driver is the comprehensive sanctions regime, particularly from the U.S. (re-imposed after its withdrawal from the JCPOA in 2018), which severely restricts oil exports, freezes foreign assets, and cuts off access to the global financial system. This cripples the government's main revenue source and hinders trade and investment.

2.  **Structural Mismanagement & Corruption:** Deep-rooted issues like widespread state intervention, inefficient subsidies, corruption within powerful institutions (like the IRGC's economic empire), and a lack of transparent governance create massive distortions, stifle private sector growth, and lead to enormous economic waste.

3.  **Chronic Inflation & Currency Collapse:** Years of monetizing fiscal deficits (printing money to cover budget shortfalls) and a lack of foreign currency inflows have led to persistent hyperinflation. The Iranian rial has lost the vast majority of its value, destroying savings and purchasing power, which in turn fuels social unrest.

4.  **Regional Instability & Military Spending:** Iran's significant investment in regional proxy groups and its ballistic missile program diverts enormous resources from productive domestic investment and social services. This spending, coupled with geopolitical tensions, further isolates Iran economically and scares off potential investors.

5.  **Over-dependence on Oil & Lack of Diversification:** Despite decades of rhetoric, the economy remains fundamentally tied to oil and gas revenues, which sanctions have drastically reduced. Non-oil sectors are underdeveloped, leaving the economy highly vulnerable to external shocks and unable to generate sufficient employment for its young population.

**Important Note:** My information cuts off in May 2025. The situation may have evolved since then, potentially through temporary sanctions relief, shifts in oil smuggling networks, or new trade agreements with partners like China or Russia. For the most current picture, I recommend consulting up-to-date reports from sources like the IMF, World Bank, or specialized economic analysts tracking Iran.
